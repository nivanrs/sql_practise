A word that appears once is noise. A word that appears a hundred times is a signal.

💻 SQL of the Day: Word Count in Drafts

Count how many times each word appears across all drafts in google_file_store. Output each word and its count, ordered by frequency descending.

─────────────────────────────────

🧠 SQL Solution

with raw as (
  select
    LOWER(REGEXP_REPLACE(word, '[^a-zA-Z0-9]', '', 'g')) AS word
    from google_file_store,
    UNNEST(STRING_TO_ARRAY(contents, ' ')) AS word
)

select 
    word, 
    count(*) as occurrences
from raw
group by word
order by 2 desc

─────────────────────────────────

🧩 Simple logic breakdown

→ STRING_TO_ARRAY splits each document on spaces into an array of tokens
→ UNNEST explodes that array so every token becomes its own row
→ REGEXP_REPLACE strips punctuation so "word." and "word" don't count as two different words
→ LOWER normalizes case so "Draft" and "draft" collapse into one
→ COUNT then aggregates across the cleaned tokens

─────────────────────────────────

📊 This pattern tells you:

• What vocabulary a team actually uses vs. what they intend to use
• Where attention lives in a document corpus before any model touches the data
• Whether documents are finished: heavy hedge words like "maybe" or "pending" signal unresolved decisions, not finished work
• The same output feeds a wordcloud directly

─────────────────────────────────

🎯 Key takeaways

1. Clean before you split. REGEXP_REPLACE on a full string is predictable. On a fragment after UNNEST, punctuation attached without a space survives.
2. LOWER and REGEXP_REPLACE together are the minimum for any word frequency task. Either one alone still produces fragmented counts.
3. Word frequency is a proxy for attention. What appears most is what people kept coming back to, regardless of what the document was supposed to cover.
