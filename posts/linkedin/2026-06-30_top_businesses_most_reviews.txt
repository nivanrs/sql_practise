You used the wrong ranking function. The query ran fine. The leaderboard was wrong.

💻 SQL of the Day: Top Businesses with Most Reviews

Problem: Find the top 5 businesses with the most reviews. Return the business name and total review count.

─────────────────────────────

🧠 SQL Solution

WITH business_rank AS (
    SELECT
        business_id,
        name,
        SUM(review_count) AS review_count,
        RANK() OVER (ORDER BY SUM(review_count) DESC) AS rnk
    FROM yelp_business
    GROUP BY business_id, name
)

SELECT name, review_count
FROM business_rank
WHERE rnk <= 5;

─────────────────────────────

🧩 Logic Breakdown
→ SUM(review_count) grouped by business gives total reviews per business.
→ RANK() OVER (ORDER BY total DESC) ranks them. Two businesses tied at rank 3 push the next to rank 5, not 4.
→ WHERE rnk <= 5 keeps everyone in the top 5 positions, including any tied at the boundary.

─────────────────────────────

📊 Business Impact
• Public leaderboards have to be fair: if two businesses tie for 5th, dropping one makes a business that earned its spot vanish from a ranking customers and partners actually see.
• Spend follows the list: featured placement and partnership outreach get aimed at this top 5, so a silently truncated leaderboard points budget at the wrong accounts.
• Competitive signal: a business tracking its own rank needs to know when it is tied with a rival, not just its raw position.

─────────────────────────────

🎯 Key Takeaways
1. ROW_NUMBER() on a tied leaderboard is a silent bug: clean output, missing entries.
2. RANK() is the right default for competitive top-N. Tied rows share a position and WHERE rnk <= 5 catches all of them.
3. DENSE_RANK() is not a safe swap. It can return more rows than expected when ties sit above the cutoff.

💬 RANK() or DENSE_RANK() for your top-N queries?
