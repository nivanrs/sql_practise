Calculating total compensation in SQL is easy until some employees have multiple bonuses and others have none.

💻 𝗦𝗤𝗟 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗗𝗮𝘆: Income By Title and Gender (StrataScratch)
🔗 Link in comments

Problem: Find the average total compensation (salary + bonus) for employees, grouped by title and gender. Only include employees who received at least one bonus.

─────────────────────────────

🧠 𝗦𝗤𝗟 𝗦𝗼𝗹𝘂𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻

WITH cte_total_bonus AS (
    SELECT
        worker_ref_id,
        SUM(bonus) AS total_bonus
    FROM sf_bonus
    GROUP BY worker_ref_id
)
SELECT
    e.employee_title,
    e.sex,
    AVG(e.salary + b.total_bonus) AS average_compensation
FROM sf_employee e
INNER JOIN cte_total_bonus b
    ON e.id = b.worker_ref_id
GROUP BY 1, 2;

─────────────────────────────

🧩 𝗦𝗶𝗺𝗽𝗹𝗲 𝗹𝗼𝗴𝗶𝗰 𝗯𝗿𝗲𝗮𝗸𝗱𝗼𝘄𝗻:
→ First, group all bonuses per employee. We use a CTE to sum them up to avoid double-counting regular salary rows.
→ Use an inner join to link those bonuses back to the main table. This automatically filters out anyone who didn't get a bonus.
→ Average the sum of salary and total bonus, grouped by role and gender.

─────────────────────────────

📊 𝗧𝗵𝗶𝘀 𝗽𝗮𝘁𝘁𝗲𝗿𝗻 𝘁𝗲𝗹𝗹𝘀 𝘆𝗼𝘂:
• These averages provide a quick snapshot of the pay structure by role and gender.
• Averages can be misleading. To see the full picture, you really need to check min/max values and headcount.
• Run a distribution check with quartiles to see where the bulk of the team actually sits if you want to find the real pay gap.

─────────────────────────────

🎯 𝗞𝗲𝘆 𝘁𝗮𝗸𝗲𝗮𝘄𝗮𝘆𝘀:
1. Business rules are everything. Excluding non-bonus employees changes the entire set of boundaries for your logic.
2. CTEs aren't just for readability. They isolate the math before you complicate things with joins.
3. Don't settle for the mean. The real story usually lives in the distribution.
