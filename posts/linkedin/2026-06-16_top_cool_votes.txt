My SQL query returned the correct result… but it was still wrong.

💻 SQL of the Day: Top Cool Votes

Problem: Find the review text with the highest number of cool votes. Return the business name and the review text.

─────────────────────────────

🧠 SQL Solution

WITH rank_list AS (
    SELECT
        business_name,
        review_text,
        cool,
        DENSE_RANK() OVER (ORDER BY cool DESC) AS rnk
    FROM yelp_reviews
)

SELECT
    business_name,
    review_text
FROM rank_list
WHERE rnk = 1;

─────────────────────────────

🧩 Logic Breakdown
→ DENSE_RANK() OVER (ORDER BY cool DESC) ranks every review, highest votes first.
→ Ties share a rank: two reviews with 10 cool votes both get rank 1, not rank 1 and rank 2.
→ WHERE rnk = 1 returns every top review, not one arbitrary row.

─────────────────────────────

📊 Business Impact
• Content discovery: platforms surface "top" content with queries like this. Drop a tie and a valid recommendation never reaches users.
• A tied business loses visibility it earned, and the product ends up recommending less-liked content while looking correct.
• Editorial trust: a "most loved review" feature that shows one of several tied winners looks arbitrary the moment a reader spots an equal review left out.

─────────────────────────────

🎯 Key Takeaways
1. ROW_NUMBER() forces unique numbers (1, 2, 3, 4) and silently drops tied rows.
2. RANK() keeps ties but skips ranks (1, 1, 3, 4). DENSE_RANK() keeps ties with no gap (1, 1, 2, 3).
3. For "all rows tied at the top," DENSE_RANK() works, and so does WHERE cool = (SELECT MAX(cool) FROM yelp_reviews).
