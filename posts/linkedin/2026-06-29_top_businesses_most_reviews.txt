You used the wrong ranking function. The query ran fine. The leaderboard was wrong.

💻 SQL of the Day: Top Businesses with Most Reviews

─────────────────────────────────

Problem:

Find the top 5 businesses with the most reviews. Return the business name and total review count.

─────────────────────────────────

🧠 SQL Solution

WITH business_rank AS (
    SELECT
        business_id,
        name,
        SUM(review_count) AS review_count,
        RANK() OVER (ORDER BY SUM(review_count) DESC) AS rnk
    FROM yelp_business
    GROUP BY business_id, name
)

SELECT name, review_count
FROM business_rank
WHERE rnk <= 5;

─────────────────────────────────

🧩 Simple logic breakdown

→ SUM(review_count) grouped by business gives total reviews per business
→ RANK() assigns position by descending count. Two businesses with the same total get rank 3. The next rank is 5, not 4.
→ WHERE rnk <= 5 keeps every business within the top 5 positions, including any tied at the boundary

─────────────────────────────────

📊 This pattern tells you:

• ROW_NUMBER() would return exactly 5 rows and look fine. But if two businesses tie at position 4, SQL drops one arbitrarily. A business that earned its spot on the Yelp leaderboard just disappears. No error, no warning.
• DENSE_RANK() has the opposite problem. It handles ties, but compresses rank numbers. Two businesses tied at rank 3 means the next rank is 4, not 5. With a top-5 filter, an extra business squeezes in at rank 5 that RANK() would exclude. You get more rows than the business expects.
• The question tells you which function to use, not habit. "Top 5 positions" calls for RANK(). "Top 5 distinct score levels" calls for DENSE_RANK(). "Give every row a unique number" calls for ROW_NUMBER().

─────────────────────────────────

🎯 Key takeaways

1. ROW_NUMBER() on a tied leaderboard is a silent bug. It returns clean output, passes every visual check, and still drops valid entries.
2. RANK() is the right call for competitive rankings. Tied businesses share the same position, and WHERE rnk <= 5 catches every one of them.
3. DENSE_RANK() can quietly expand top-N results past what you expect. When ties exist above the cutoff, compressed numbering lets lower-ranked entries slip through.
